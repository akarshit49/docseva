# Strategy v2 — Vertical Beachhead: Who We Actually Sell To, and Why They Pay

> **Supersedes** the previous strategy doc's horizontal "WhatsApp quoting for all MSMEs +
> payment-link" thesis. Two critiques killed parts of that thesis, and they were right:
>
> 1. **No one pays via WhatsApp in professional B2B.** B2B India = PO → invoice → NEFT/
>    RTGS/cheque on 30–90 day credit. The payment-link revenue lever is dropped.
> 2. **The segmentation paradox:** "uneducated MSMEs don't need quotes; educated ones
>    already own billing software — so who's left?" This is a real trap for any
>    *horizontal* MSME play.
>
> This document fixes both by going **vertical**, and reconciles the "should we keep the
> other features?" question.
>
> **Date:** 1 June 2026

---

## Part 1 — The segmentation paradox, answered

The critique: *uneducated MSMEs don't need a quotation system; educated ones already have
billing software; so there's no one to sell to.*

It feels airtight. It isn't. It has two holes.

### Hole 1 — "Has billing software" ≠ "quoting pain solved"

Tally, Vyapar, Busy, Zoho, myBillBook are **accounting / GST / inventory** tools, usually
operated by the **accountant**. But a **quotation** — especially a complex, multi-line,
spec-heavy, reformatted-from-a-supplier, tender-format quotation — is **still made in Word,
Excel, or by hand**, by the **salesperson or proprietor**, *even at firms that own billing
software.* The two jobs barely overlap. Owning Tally does not make quoting fast or
professional.

### Hole 2 — the variable is not "education," it's "quoting intensity & complexity"

The real buyer is defined by a **workflow**, not a literacy level:

> A business whose **revenue depends on sending many non-trivial quotes**, who often
> **reuses/reformats a supplier's quote into their own**, and for whom **being fast and
> looking professional wins deals.**

### Conclusion

The paradox is only fatal if we stay **horizontal** ("quoting for all MSMEs" = the
squeezed middle = the SaaS graveyard). The escape is to go **vertical**: own one trade
where this workflow is the daily lifeblood, then expand to adjacent trades that share the
exact pattern.

## Part 2 — The beachhead we already own

The founder comes from **scientific / lab / industrial instrument trading** (Sanmati,
Roorkee). This is not a random niche — it is a near-perfect beachhead:

- The **dealer/reseller/system-integrator** model dominates: they buy from an OEM/principal/
  distributor and re-sell to end customers (govt labs, universities, hospitals, factories,
  R&D centres).
- Quotes are **complex** — many line items, make/model, technical specs, HSN, GST, often
  in **rigid tender formats**.
- The **#1 daily job**: take a supplier's quote and **re-quote it to the end customer**
  with your markup, your branding, your format. (This is exactly the "sister quotation"
  behavior — which is *core here*, not niche.)
- The founder has **domain credibility and warm distribution** (Sanmati + Roorkee dealer
  WhatsApp groups + industry contacts). Near-zero CAC to start.

This single niche generalizes to a meaningful TAM via **adjacent verticals with the same
pattern**: medical devices, industrial machinery & spares, electrical/electronic
components, IT hardware / AV / security system integrators, safety equipment, lab consumables,
building materials with specs. Classic *beachhead → adjacent expansion.*

## Part 3 — Who we are FOR and NOT for (say it out loud)

| | |
|---|---|
| **FOR** | Technical-product **dealers / resellers / system integrators** who quote frequently, reformat supplier quotes, and chase institutional/tender business. |
| **NOT for** | Tiny shops that don't quote (kirana, retail counters). They have no need. |
| **NOT for** | Large firms with full ERP/CRM. They're already served. |

Naming our non-customers protects focus. The squeezed middle dies trying to serve everyone.

## Part 4 — Do we scrap the other features? No — we DEMOTE them.

The original mistake was **leading with all 12 features as equals**, so there was no
anchor and nobody could say what the product *is*. The fix is hierarchy, not deletion.

### Tier 1 — The Anchor (the one thing the product "is")

> **Turn a supplier's quote (or a rough item list) into your own fast, professional,
> branded customer quotation.**

This is the headline. This is onboarding. This is the demo.

### Tier 2 — The Coherent Vertical Toolkit (supports the anchor)

These belong because *this trade actually does all of them*:

| Feature | Role in the workflow |
|---|---|
| Sister quotation (reformat supplier → your brand) | The anchor itself |
| Quotation → Bill / Invoice | Closing a won deal |
| Quotation comparison | Choosing between supplier offers / procurement |
| GST validation | Tender & institutional compliance safety |
| Complex multi-item / HSN / spec quotes | The nature of technical selling |

### Tier 3 — The Utilities Drawer (keep, but hide from the pitch)

Watermark, background removal, product catalog PDF, PDF↔DOCX↔Excel conversion, rename.

- **Keep them** — they cost almost nothing to maintain and create occasional delight and
  stickiness ("oh, it can also do this").
- **But they NEVER appear in the pitch or onboarding.** They live in a "More tools"
  drawer, discovered later.

> A Swiss Army knife is **sold as a knife.** The other tools are a pleasant surprise, not
> the sales pitch. This gives you the "handy multi-tool" value *without* the
> "I-don't-get-what-this-is" problem that got the bad review.

## Part 5 — What changed about channel & money (correcting v1)

### Channel — don't be dogmatic about WhatsApp-as-everything

- Sharing the **final quote PDF on WhatsApp** is fine and normal — everyone does it.
- But the **tool itself** does not have to live entirely inside WhatsApp chat. A dead-simple
  interface (could be WhatsApp, could be a minimal web/app) where you build the quote and
  then share/print the PDF however you want (WhatsApp, email, print) is more honest.
- **Let validation decide the interface.** Don't over-engineer a channel thesis.

### Money — drop the payment-link lever

B2B doesn't pay via WhatsApp links. Revenue is **subscription**, justified by clear ROI:
time saved on complex quotes + more deals won by being fast and professional. Optional
future levers (later, only if earned): tender/lead intelligence, OEM–dealer quote flows.
Do **not** build the business model on payment fees.

---

## Part 6 — Why they'll actually pay (better economics than v1)

In this vertical the willingness-to-pay case is far stronger than generic micro-retail:

- Ticket sizes are large (quotes of ₹50k–₹50L); a single won tender dwarfs any subscription.
- Looking professional vs. competitors **directly affects who wins** institutional deals.
- Saving hours on a multi-page tender quote is real, repeated time.
- The buyer (dealer/proprietor) personally feels this pain — it's not delegated to a CA.

Pricing stays simple (two tiers, e.g. ₹499 and ₹1,499/mo). The number must be a 10-second
decision, justified by "you'll win one more deal a year." Still, **WTP must be proven, not
assumed** (Part 7).

## Part 7 — Validate in the niche (same discipline, sharper target)

The advantage now: the validation audience **is** the founder's own network.

**Gate 1 — Discovery (1 week, no code).** Interview **15–20 instrument/equipment dealers**
you can reach (Sanmati network + Roorkee groups). Mom-Test style:
- "Walk me through the last quote you sent a customer. Where did the base numbers come from?"
- "How often do you reformat a supplier's quote into your own? How long does it take?"
- "What did the last tender quote cost you in time? What goes wrong?"
- "What do you use today — Word, Excel, Tally? What do you pay for?"

> **KILL if** <10 of them describe complex/reformatted quoting as a real, recurring,
> time-and-deal-costing pain **unprompted.**

**Gate 2 — Paid concierge (2–3 weeks, almost no code).** For 5–10 dealers: they send you a
supplier quote / item list; you return a branded, tender-ready quote within the hour using
the existing engine, manually. **Charge a token fee.**

> **KILL if** <~40% will pay a small monthly fee after experiencing it, or usage fades.

Because the target is your own trade, both gates are cheap and fast. You can walk away in
weeks if it's not real.

## Part 8 — If validated, what we reuse vs. build

The hardest asset already exists and is *more* valuable under the vertical framing:

| Keep (already built) | Now used for |
|---|---|
| LLM parsing of messy input → structured items | "Supplier quote / rough list → structured quote" |
| Branded PDF/DOCX renderer (logo, GST, Indian number words) | Professional, tender-ready customer quotes |
| Sister-quotation reformatting + saved templates | The anchor feature (now headline, not buried) |
| Quotation comparison, GST validation | Tier-2 toolkit |
| Multi-tenant profile/branding/quota | Accounts & auto-branding |

Build/refocus:
- Make **sister quotation the front door**, not one of twelve buttons.
- Tender-format templates for the niche (a real differentiator vs. generic invoicers).
- Demote utilities to a hidden drawer.
- Keep the interface dead simple; pick channel based on Gate 1–2 evidence.

## Part 9 — Honest risks (updated)

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| The niche is too small | It's a *beachhead*; the pattern repeats across adjacent technical-trade verticals |
| Dealers are happy with Word/Excel and won't pay | Exactly what Gate 1–2 test; kill if so |
| Incumbents add quoting | They're accounting-first and horizontal; we're quoting-first and vertical-deep with tender formats they ignore |
| AI puts a wrong price/spec in a quote | Mandatory confirm-before-send; show extracted data; this trade is unforgiving on errors |
| Founder over-builds the toolkit again | This doc's hierarchy (Anchor / Toolkit / hidden Utilities) is the guardrail |

## One-paragraph summary

The "who do we sell to?" paradox only kills a **horizontal** MSME quoting play. We escape
it by going **vertical**: own the trade the founder already knows — **scientific / lab /
industrial instrument dealers** — where complex, supplier-reformatted, tender-format
quoting is the daily lifeblood and is *not* solved by the Tally/Vyapar accounting software
they may already own. We **don't scrap** the other features — we put them in a hierarchy:
**one anchor** (turn a supplier quote into your branded customer quote fast), a **coherent
Tier-2 toolkit** (quote→bill, comparison, GST check), and a **hidden utilities drawer**
(watermark, bg-removal, catalog, conversions) that delights but never appears in the pitch.
We **drop** the WhatsApp-payment thesis (B2B doesn't pay that way) and monetize with simple
subscriptions justified by deals-won and hours-saved. And we **validate in the founder's own
network with real money** before building — kill fast if the pain or the willingness to pay
isn't there.
